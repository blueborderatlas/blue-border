# Walking Through the Real King’s Landing — Dubrovnik

Before coming to Dubrovnik, I had just started rewatching *Game of Thrones* again.

Around that time, my social media feeds kept pushing videos about the “real-life King’s Landing.” The same images appeared over and over again: orange rooftops, massive stone walls, narrow medieval streets, and a city surrounded entirely by the sea. It almost looked unreal.

One day, I came across a sentence online:

*"Do you want to see the real King’s Landing with your own eyes?"*

That was the moment I seriously started searching for Dubrovnik.

The first time I saw photos of the city, it was honestly difficult to think of it as a normal place where people still live their daily lives. From a distance, the old town looks almost fictional. Thick stone walls surround the entire city, red-orange rooftops stretch toward the Adriatic Sea, and sunlight reflects everywhere from the water and limestone streets.

It feels more like a fantasy harbor from another world than a modern European city.

Because of this atmosphere, Dubrovnik later became one of the main filming locations for King’s Landing in *Game of Thrones*.

Many iconic scenes were filmed here.

The Jesuit Staircase inside the old town became the setting for Cersei’s famous “Walk of Shame.” Fort Lovrijenac, standing outside the walls above the sea, was used as part of the Red Keep. Even many small alleys, stone gates, stairways, and hidden corners throughout the city appeared in the series.

Standing there in person, it becomes very easy to understand why the producers chose Dubrovnik as the political center of Westeros.

But after actually entering the old town, I realized the most impressive part of Dubrovnik is not that it looks like a TV show.

It is the city itself.

Almost the entire old town is built from pale limestone. During the middle of the day, sunlight reflects intensely from the stone streets and walls, making the whole city almost glow. The narrow alleyways, endless staircases, and sudden views of the sea create a strange feeling that is difficult to explain.

It does not feel like a modern city.

It feels like a medieval port that somehow survived into the present day.

What makes Dubrovnik especially interesting is how quickly its atmosphere changes throughout the day.

During the daytime, the main streets become extremely crowded. Cruise ship tourists flood into the old town, many searching specifically for *Game of Thrones* filming locations. Shops advertise “Game of Thrones Tours” almost everywhere, and the bright sunlight reflecting from the white stone can make the city feel overwhelming.

But the moment you leave the main streets and enter the smaller side alleys, everything suddenly becomes quiet.

Laundry hangs between old buildings. You hear televisions playing through open windows. The smell of dinner drifts through narrow stairways, and local residents sit outside talking in the evening air.

At that moment, Dubrovnik stops feeling like a movie set.

It becomes a real city again.

In the evening, the atmosphere changes completely once more.

The stone walls slowly release the heat collected during the day, sea wind moves through the streets, and the old town becomes softer and calmer. Many people sit near the rocks outside the walls watching the sunset disappear into the Adriatic Sea.

Compared to the busy daytime energy, Dubrovnik at night feels much more authentic. Small restaurants place tables inside narrow alleys, warm lights reflect against ancient stone walls, and simply walking through the city starts to feel cinematic without even trying.

The sea is always present here.

No matter where you stand, you can almost always hear waves crashing against the walls below the city. Many buildings look as if they grew directly out of the cliffs and rocks beside the water. This constant connection between the city and the sea is part of what makes Dubrovnik feel different from many other historic European towns.

Maybe that is why Dubrovnik always feels caught somewhere between reality and fantasy.

It feels both real and fictional at the same time. Historical, but also strangely cinematic.

Many people come here because of *Game of Thrones*.

But what stays in memory afterward is usually not the series itself.

It is the atmosphere of the city. The stone streets, the sea air, the medieval walls, and the feeling that for a short moment, you stepped into something that does not fully belong to the modern world.
